# Legal Framework: Existing Lawful Water Use under the National Water Act

**DWA V&V System — Legal Reference Document**
**Based on**: National Water Act (Act 38 of 1998); *Requirements for Verifying the Lawfulness of Existing Water Use and Extent of Existing Lawful Water Use*, Edition 3, July 2024 (DWS)

---

## 1. Why This Document Exists

The Validation & Verification (V&V) process is fundamentally a **legal process** — it determines whether a person's water use is lawful, and if so, how much water they are entitled to. Every decision the system records, every letter it generates, and every calculation it performs traces back to specific provisions of the National Water Act. This document explains those provisions in plain language and shows how they connect to the system we are building.

---

## 2. The Historical Context

### 2.1 The Old Regime (1956 Water Act)

Under the Water Act of 1956, water rights were tied to land ownership — the **riparian principle**. If you owned land alongside a river, you had rights to that water. Groundwater on your property was considered private water. This system:

- Favoured existing landowners (predominantly white, due to apartheid-era land allocation)
- Made no provision for equitable access or ecological sustainability
- Did not treat water as a single, interconnected national resource

### 2.2 The New Regime (National Water Act, 1998)

The NWA, which commenced on **1 October 1998**, introduced three fundamental changes:

1. **Decoupling water from land** — Section 4(4) replaced old water rights with entitlements under the new Act. The Minister of Water Affairs and Forestry became the custodian of the entire water resource.

2. **Water as a public resource** — All water is linked in a single naturally occurring water cycle. Water found on one property is connected to water everywhere else. The nation's needs may take priority.

3. **Equity and transformation** — Licences must give effect to the purposes of the Act, including addressing race and gender transformation and meeting the needs of the Reserve (the ecological and basic human needs allocation).

### 2.3 The Transition Problem

It was impractical to force every water user in the country to immediately apply for a new licence. So the NWA created a **transitional mechanism**: uses that were lawful before the Act could continue as a special category of entitlement called **Existing Lawful Water Use (ELU)**, until replaced by a licence through compulsory licensing.

The V&V system exists to **verify** which uses qualify as ELU, **determine the extent** (volume) of that use, and **issue the legal notices** required by the Act.

## 3. The Four Water Use Authorisation Types

Under the NWA, all water use must fall into one of four categories. These form a hierarchy from least to most regulated:

| # | Authorisation Type | Description | Registration Required? | Licence Required? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Schedule 1 Use** | Small domestic use: washing, cooking, drinking, small food gardens, watering non-commercial livestock | No | No |
| 2 | **General Authorisation (GA)** | Volumes above Schedule 1 but within limits set per catchment by Government Gazette. GA volumes vary by area based on water availability | Registration with CMA/DWS may be required | No |
| 3 | **Existing Lawful Water Use (ELU)** | Water use lawful before the NWA that was exercised during the qualifying period. Continues until replaced by a licence. A Section 35(4) certificate has the same legal status as a licence | Was required by Nov 1999 (WARMS) | No (until compulsory licensing) |
| 4 | **Licensed Water Use** | Any use exceeding GA, or any use after compulsory licensing replaces ELU | Yes | Yes (90-day processing target) |

**Key point**: An ELU certificate issued under Section 35(4) carries the **same legal weight as a licence**. It is not a lesser entitlement — it is a full, legally recognised authorisation to use water.

## 4. Sections 32–35: The Legal Chain

These four sections of the NWA work together as a logical chain. Each builds on the previous one:

### 4.1 Section 32 — Definition of Existing Lawful Water Use

> *"An existing lawful water use means a water use which has taken place at any time during a period of two years immediately before the date of commencement of this Act, and which was authorised by or under any law which was in force immediately before the date of commencement of this Act."*

**In plain language**: If you were using water during the **qualifying period** (the 2 years before 1 October 1998), and that use was legal under whatever law applied at the time (the 1956 Water Act, permits, or uses that didn't require authorisation), then that water use qualifies as ELU.

**What qualifies under the old law**:
- Permits issued under the 1956 Water Act
- Taking water for agricultural purposes (Section 21(a) of the 1956 Act) — irrigation
- Storing water in dams (Section 21(b)) — impoundments
- Stream flow reduction activities / forestry (Section 21(d))
- Any use that was lawfully exercised without needing a permit under the old Act

### 4.2 Section 33 — Declaration of ELU (The Safety Net)

Section 33 allows the responsible authority to **declare** certain water uses as ELU even when they don't strictly meet the Section 32 definition. This covers two specific situations:

#### Exception 1: Discontinued for Good Reason

A water use that was lawful and took place **before the start of the qualifying period**, but was discontinued before or during the qualifying period for a **good reason**.

**What constitutes "good reason"**:
- Financial constraints of the owner
- Equipment breakdown requiring repair
- Death of the owner
- Drought or hydrological conditions
- Other factual circumstances beyond the user's control

**What does NOT constitute good reason**:
- Voluntary abandonment of the water use
- Sale of the property followed by non-use by the new owner
- Simply choosing not to irrigate for commercial reasons

The test is **restrictive and objective** — the reason must relate directly to why the use was not exercised during the qualifying period, assessed against all surrounding circumstances. Section 33 is not a mechanism to revive long-abandoned water use or to create new entitlements for new landowners.

#### Exception 2: Steps Taken in Good Faith

A water use that **never actually took place** (including uses that had not started at all), but:
- It **would have been lawful** if it had taken place, AND
- **Reasonable steps** had been taken toward implementing it **before** the NWA commenced

**Examples of "reasonable steps"**:
- Installing irrigation equipment
- Establishing plantations (for forestry/SFRA)
- Obtaining engineering designs for a dam
- Entering into contracts for irrigation infrastructure

The "good faith" test is also **restrictive and objective**. Planning a project or merely intending to use water is not sufficient — tangible, demonstrable steps must have been taken before 1 October 1998.

#### Important Limitations on Section 33

- It is **NOT** a way to revive long-unused water rights as an alternative to buying land with active water entitlements
- It is **NOT** a way to verify the lawfulness of a water use — that requires Section 35
- Only **circumstances that existed before 1 October 1998** may be considered
- The procedure to declare a water use as ELU under Section 33 should **not be merged** with the Section 35 verification procedure — they are separate processes
- Each application must be considered on its own merits

> **Note**: Proposed amendments to the NWA (Government Gazette 49733, 17 November 2023) propose repealing Section 33, which would mean only uses that *actually took place* during the qualifying period could qualify as ELU. At the time of writing (2024), this amendment has not yet been enacted.

### 4.3 Section 34 — Authority to Continue

> *Section 34 provides the authority to continue with an existing lawful use — under the same conditions — until its replacement by a licence.*

This is the **bridge provision**. It ensures that verified ELU holders can legally continue using water while the country transitions to the new licensing regime. Without Section 34, there would be a legal gap between the old and new systems.

**Key conditions**:
- The use must continue under the **same conditions** as it existed — you cannot increase your use
- It continues **until replaced by a licence** — this happens during compulsory licensing
- Trading an ELU also converts it to a licence (subject to Section 27 conditions)

### 4.4 Section 35 — Verification of Existing Lawful Water Use

This is the **operational section** that our system implements. It provides the legal basis for the entire V&V process.

**What Section 35 empowers**:

1. **Initiation**: The responsible authority (DWS or CMA) may require persons claiming ELU to **apply for verification** of their water use. This can be done:
   - For **all** water users in a geographic area (catchment-wide approach)
   - For **some** water users (targeted verification)
   - On **individual request** (e.g., property sale, dispute resolution)

2. **Scope**: Verification determines both:
   - The **existence** of the ELU (is this use lawful at all?)
   - The **extent** of the ELU (what volume of water is the user entitled to?)

3. **Obligation to comply**: Persons who **do not apply** for verification when requested **may not continue** to use the water. This is a critical enforcement point.

4. **Refusal**: If a verification application is **refused**, the user must stop using the water.

5. **Public participation**: Section 35 also allows for **anyone affected** by the process to provide input — this includes neighbouring water users, environmental groups, and new licence applicants who have been told no water is available.

6. **Appeals**: Water users who are dissatisfied with the outcome may raise appeals to the **Water Tribunal**.

7. **Certification**: A successful verification results in a **Section 35(4) certificate** — this is the formal confirmation of the user's ELU and its extent. It has the same legal status as a licence.

---

## 5. Section 53 — Directives (Enforcement)

Section 53 is **separate from** Sections 32–35. It is not about defining or verifying lawful use — it is the **enforcement mechanism** that gives DWS the power to compel compliance.

**When Section 53 applies in the V&V context**:

| Situation | What DWS does | Legal basis |
|---|---|---|
| Water user ignores Letter 1 (S35(1) notice to apply) | Issues Letter 1A — a **directive** compelling the user to apply | S53(1) |
| Water user ignores Letter 2 (S35(3)(a) request for information) | Issues Letter 2A — a **directive** compelling the user to provide information | S35(1) read with S53(1) |
| Verification reveals **unlawful** water use | Issues Letter 4A — **notice of intent** to direct the user to stop | S53(1) |
| After Letter 4A notice period expires | Issues Letters 4 & 5 — **directive to cease** unlawful water use | S53(1) |

**The distinction**: Section 35 is the process; Section 53 is the stick. Section 35 asks. Section 53 compels.

## 6. The Qualifying Period

### 6.1 Definition

The "qualifying period" is the **2 years immediately before the commencement of the NWA** (1 October 1998). Water use that was lawful and exercised during this window may qualify as ELU.

### 6.2 Dates by Water Source

| Water Source | Qualifying Period | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Surface water** (rivers, canals, irrigation schemes) | **1 October 1997 – 1 October 1999** | Section 32(1) — 2 years before commencement |
| **Groundwater** (boreholes) | **1 October 1996 – 1 October 1998** | Groundwater outside proclaimed Government Water Control Areas was "private water" under the 1956 Act; the relevant period is therefore 2 years before 1 October 1998 |

> **Note on surface water dates**: The surface water qualifying period extends to 1 October 1999 (one year *after* the NWA commenced) because Section 32(1) refers to use "at any time during a period of two years immediately before the date of commencement" — and the registration process required users to indicate their use 2–3 years after the qualifying period. The practical qualifying period assessment window captures use that was occurring around the commencement date.

### 6.3 Why the Qualifying Period Matters for the System

The qualifying period is the **evidentiary anchor** for every V&V determination:

| System Activity | Qualifying Period Relevance |
|---|---|
| **Satellite imagery** | Images must originate from or relate to the qualifying period. More recent images may supplement but cannot replace qualifying-period evidence. |
| **GIS digitising** | Crop fields and dams are digitised for BOTH the qualifying period AND the current period. The delta determines lawful vs. unlawful use. |
| **SAPWAT modelling** | Crop water requirements are calculated based on what was planted during the qualifying period. |
| **ELU volume calculation** | The lawful volume is based on qualifying-period use. Any increase beyond that volume is a potential unlawful increase. |
| **Dam volume calculation** | Dam capacity that existed during the qualifying period determines the lawful storage entitlement. |
| **Forestation/SFRA** | Forestry that existed within the qualifying period has ELU regardless of whether it had a forestry permit (confirmed by 2024 Constitutional Court ruling). |

### 6.4 The Critical Rule

> **Only circumstances and factors that existed prior to the commencement of the NWA (1 October 1998) may be considered** in determining whether a water use is an ELU.

Any subsequent behaviour by a water user — expanding irrigation, building new dams, planting additional crops — is **irrelevant** to the ELU determination, except insofar as it helps clarify what the situation was before the NWA commenced.

If a water user violates a condition of their ELU (e.g., uses more water than entitled), steps may be taken to rectify the matter, but the ELU itself is determined as it existed on the commencement date.

## 8. The Section 35 Letter Sequence

After the technical V&V work is complete (control points 1–9), the case enters the **Section 35 Validation Process** — a formal legal communication sequence with the water user.

### 8.1 Letter Types and Legal Basis

| Letter | NWA Section | Purpose | Delivery Method |
|--------|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| **Letter 1** | S35(1) | Notice to the water user to apply for verification of their ELU | **Must be served in person** (NWA Section 162) |
| **Letter 1A** | S53(1) | Directive compelling the user to apply (issued when Letter 1 gets no response) | Registered post or as agreed |
| **Letter 2** | S35(3)(a) | Request for additional information needed to complete verification | As agreed with user |
| **Letter 2A** | S35(1) | Directive to provide the requested information (issued when Letter 2 gets no response) | Registered post or as agreed |
| **Letter 3** | S35(4) | **ELU Certificate** — formal confirmation of the existence and extent of the user's lawful water use | Registered post or as agreed |
| **Letter 4A** | S53(1) | Notice of intent to issue a directive to stop unlawful water use | Registered post |
| **Letters 4 & 5** | S53(1) | **Directive to cease** unlawful water use | Registered post |

### 8.2 Letter 1 — Special Service Requirements

Letter 1 is the only letter that **legally must be served in person** (Section 162 of the NWA). The "in person" requirement means delivery by:

- An official of the Department (DWS)
- Their appointed agent (including consultants)
- A sheriff of the court

**For a natural person** — the service hierarchy is:
1. Hand delivery to the person themselves
2. If not possible → hand delivery to a responsible individual at their business or residential address
3. If not possible → registered mail to their business or residential address
4. If address unknown → publish in the Government Gazette AND a local newspaper

**For a juristic person** (company, trust, etc.) — the service hierarchy is:
1. Hand delivery to a responsible individual at the registered address or principal place of business
2. If not possible → fax to the registered address or principal place of business
3. If not possible → registered mail to the registered address or principal place of business
4. If not possible → conspicuously attach to the main entrance of the registered address
5. If not possible → hand delivery to any member of the board of directors or governing body

Upon serving Letter 1, the method of **further communication** should be agreed with the user and recorded in the file. Only Letter 1 has this strict in-person requirement — subsequent letters may be delivered by registered post, email (with proof of delivery), or through the portal.

## 9. The PAJA Dimension

The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA, Act 3 of 2000) governs all administrative decisions in South Africa, including ELU determinations. This is significant because:

- Every ELU determination is an **administrative action** — it must be **lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair**
- Water users whose rights are adversely affected have a constitutional right to be **given reasons**
- Court and tribunal cases against DWS have mostly been lost due to **procedural failures**, not substantive errors
- The V&V process must therefore meticulously follow procedure and maintain a complete audit trail

**What this means for the system**:
- Every state transition, letter issuance, and decision must be recorded in the immutable audit log
- The water user must have opportunity to respond at each stage (Letters 1, 2, and before Letter 3)
- The system must support the appeal process (Water Tribunal referral after Letter 3)
- All calculations and evidence must be traceable — the system must be able to reconstruct *how* an ELU volume was determined

## 10. Compulsory Licensing — What Comes After V&V

V&V does not exist in isolation. It is **Phase 1** of a broader water reform process:

```
Phase 1: Verification (V&V)          ──── This system
    │
    │  Determines who has ELU
    │  and how much
    │
    ▼
Phase 2: Compulsory Licensing         ──── Future process
    │
    │  In water-stressed catchments,
    │  DWS may require ALL users
    │  (including verified ELU holders)
    │  to apply for new licences
    │
    ▼
Phase 3: Water Allocation             ──── Future process
    │
    │  DWS allocates available water
    │  among applicants, considering:
    │  • The Reserve (ecological + basic human needs)
    │  • Equity and transformation
    │  • International obligations
    │  • Existing lawful use
    │
    ▼
Outcome: New licences replace all ELUs
```

During compulsory licensing:
- ELU holders may have their volumes **reduced** if the catchment is over-allocated
- New users may receive allocations that were previously held as ELU
- The Section 35(4) certificate (Letter 3) is the user's evidence of their prior lawful use — it is a critical input to the licensing decision

This is why **accuracy in V&V is paramount** — an incorrect ELU determination affects not just the individual user, but the entire water allocation for the catchment.

## 11. Special Cases and Exceptions

### 11.1 Forestry / Stream Flow Reduction Activities (SFRA)

Forestry that existed within the qualifying period has ELU **regardless of whether it had a forestry permit**. This was:
- Required in the former RSA since 1972
- Required in former homeland areas since 1994
- Confirmed by a **2024 Constitutional Court ruling**

The system tracks SFRA separately through the `Forestation` entity, with qualifying period hectares, current hectares, and lawful/unlawful volume calculations.

### 11.2 Government and Irrigation Board Schemes

By declaration of the Minister, all scheduled areas under Government and Irrigation Board schemes as at 1 January 1999 — for which prescribed rates had been paid up to 30 September 1998 — are treated as ELU, **even if the water was not actually used during the qualifying period**. This is because paying the rates demonstrated an active entitlement.

### 11.3 Unregistered Users

Users identified through GIS analysis who have water use (visible on satellite imagery) but never registered with WARMS are flagged as "Unregistered" during V&V. They must still go through the Section 35 process — their use may or may not be lawful depending on qualifying period evidence.

### 11.4 Property Sales and Ownership Changes

ELU is attached to the **water use**, not the **person**. When a property changes hands:
- The new owner inherits the ELU (if any) attached to that property
- The new owner cannot claim a *new* ELU based on their own activities
- The V&V determination looks at what was happening on the property during the qualifying period, regardless of who owned it then

## 13. Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **CMA** | Catchment Management Agency — regional water management body established under the NWA |
| **ELU** | Existing Lawful Water Use — water use lawful before the NWA, verified under Section 35 |
| **GA** | General Authorisation — gazetted volume thresholds below which no licence is needed |
| **GWCA** | Government Water Control Area — proclaimed area under the 1956 Water Act |
| **NWA** | National Water Act (Act 38 of 1998) |
| **PAJA** | Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (Act 3 of 2000) |
| **Qualifying Period** | The 2-year window before the NWA commenced (surface: Oct 97–99; borehole: Oct 96–98) |
| **Reserve** | The quantity and quality of water required to satisfy basic human needs and protect aquatic ecosystems |
| **Riparian Principle** | Old legal principle tying water rights to land ownership — replaced by the NWA |
| **SAPWAT** | Crop water requirement model used to calculate irrigation volumes (output: mm/ha/a) |
| **SFRA** | Stream Flow Reduction Activity — primarily commercial forestry |
| **V&V** | Validation and Verification — the process of confirming ELU existence and extent |
| **WARMS** | Water Authorisation and Registration Management System — DWS's existing water use database |
| **WMA** | Water Management Area — one of 19 geographic areas for water resource management |
