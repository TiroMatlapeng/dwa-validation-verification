1. Executive Summary

The Department of Water and Sanitation (DWS) requires an electronic information management system to support the Validation and Verification (V&V) of existing lawful water use (ELU) across South Africa, as mandated by Sections 32–35 of the National Water Act (Act 38 of 1998). This document presents the solution design for the DWA V&V System.

The system will manage approximately 500,000 property records distributed across DWS provincial offices, regional offices, Water Management Areas (WMAs), Catchment Management Agencies (CMAs), and contracted service providers. The national target for completing all verification is 2026.

The solution delivers the following core capabilities:

  • Workflow Engine — A state machine that drives each property through 21 control points across three phases: Project Inception, Verification, and Validation (Section 35). The engine enforces role-based guards, evidence requirements, and sequential processing.
  • Calculator Engine — A dedicated, parameterised calculation module that performs SAPWAT crop water requirement modelling, ELU volume determination, dam volume calculation (Appendix D formulas), and stream flow reduction activity (SFRA) assessment.
  • Letter and Correspondence Engine — Generates, tracks, and manages all seven Section 35 letter types, including electronic signature capture, batch processing, and statutory deadline management.
  • Public Portal — A secure, externally-facing portal through which water users can view their case status, receive notifications, respond to letters, upload supporting documents, and lodge formal protests.
  • External System Integrations — Bi-directional integration with WARMS (Water Authorisation and Registration Management System), eWULAAS (licence applications), the Surveyor General, and the Deeds Office.
  • Audit and Compliance — An immutable audit trail that records every action, state transition, and decision, ensuring compliance with the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA).

The system is built on ASP.NET Core 10.0 with Entity Framework Core 10.0 and SQL Server 2022, aligning with DWS's existing technology environment (both WARMS and eWULAAS run on C#/ASP.NET/SQL Server). The architecture follows a layered pattern (Controllers → Services → Repositories → Database) with clear separation of concerns and dependency injection throughout.

Key Business Outcome
The system replaces the current fragmented approach — Microsoft Access databases, Excel spreadsheets, and paper-based correspondence — with a single, centralised platform that provides real-time visibility across all provinces and WMAs, enforces statutory procedures, and produces legally defensible ELU certificates.

2. Business Context and Problem Statement

2.1 Legislative Mandate

The National Water Act (Act 38 of 1998) fundamentally restructured water law in South Africa. Under the previous Water Act of 1956, water rights were tied to land ownership through the riparian principle — if you owned land alongside a river, you had rights to that water. The NWA decoupled water from land, making the Minister of Water Affairs and Forestry the custodian of all water resources.

However, it was impractical to force every water user to immediately apply for a new licence. The NWA therefore created a transitional mechanism: water uses that were lawful before the Act commenced could continue as Existing Lawful Water Use (ELU) — a special entitlement with the same legal force as a licence — until replaced through compulsory licensing.

The V&V process is the mechanism through which DWS verifies which water uses qualify as ELU and determines the extent (volume) of each entitlement. It is governed by Sections 32–35 of the NWA and enforced through Section 53 directives.

2.2 The V&V Process in Context

V&V is Phase 1 of a broader water reform process:

PhaseProcessObjective
Phase 1Validation & Verification (V&V)Determine who has ELU and how much — this system
Phase 2Compulsory LicensingIn water-stressed catchments, require all users (including ELU holders) to apply for new licences
Phase 3Water AllocationAllocate available water among applicants, considering the Reserve, equity, transformation, and international obligations

The accuracy of V&V directly affects compulsory licensing outcomes. An incorrect ELU determination does not just affect the individual water user — it distorts the entire water allocation for the catchment.

2.3 Current State and Challenges

The current V&V process relies on:

  • Microsoft Access databases maintained per catchment or per service provider, with no centralised view
  • Excel spreadsheets for calculation tracking and progress reporting
  • Paper-based correspondence for Section 35 letters, with manual tracking of delivery, due dates, and responses
  • WARMS as the registration database, but with no direct integration to V&V data
  • No public-facing capability — water users have no visibility into their case status unless they contact DWS directly

This fragmentation creates several risks:

  • No consolidated national view of V&V progress across all 19 WMAs
  • Inconsistent application of the process between regional offices and service providers
  • Difficulty demonstrating PAJA compliance when ELU determinations are challenged at the Water Tribunal
  • Loss of institutional knowledge when staff or service providers change
  • Missed statutory deadlines for letter responses, leading to legal exposure

2.4 Target State

The DWA V&V System will provide:

  • A single centralised platform accessible to all DWS offices, CMAs, and service providers
  • Workflow enforcement ensuring every property follows the mandated 21-step process
  • Automated calculations reducing manual error in ELU, dam volume, and SFRA determinations
  • Digital correspondence with full lifecycle tracking from generation through delivery to response
  • Public transparency through a secure portal giving water users direct visibility into their cases
  • Complete audit trail providing defensible evidence for Water Tribunal proceedings
  • Real-time reporting across all provinces and WMAs

3. Legal Framework

Every decision the system records, every letter it generates, and every calculation it performs traces back to specific provisions of the National Water Act. This section establishes the legal foundation that governs the system's design.

3.1 Water Use Authorisation Types

Under the NWA, all water use must fall into one of four categories, forming a hierarchy from least to most regulated:

#TypeDescriptionRegistration?Licence?
1Schedule 1 UseSmall domestic use: washing, cooking, drinking, small food gardens, non-commercial livestockNoNo
2General AuthorisationVolumes above Schedule 1 but within limits set per catchment by Government GazetteMay be requiredNo
3Existing Lawful Use (ELU)Water use lawful before the NWA, exercised during the qualifying period. A S35(4) certificate = licenceRequired (WARMS)No (until compulsory licensing)
4Licensed Water UseAny use exceeding GA, or any use after compulsory licensing replaces ELUYesYes
Key Point
An ELU certificate issued under Section 35(4) carries the same legal weight as a licence. It is not a lesser entitlement — it is a full, legally recognised authorisation to use water.

3.2 Sections 32–35: The Legal Chain

These four sections work together as a logical chain, each building on the previous one:

Section 32 — Definition of ELU

Defines existing lawful water use as a water use which has taken place at any time during a period of two years immediately before the date of commencement of this Act, and which was authorised by or under any law in force immediately before that date. This covers permits issued under the 1956 Water Act as well as uses that did not require permits (irrigation, dams, forestry).

Section 33 — Declaration of ELU (Safety Net)

Extends ELU to cover two specific situations where water use did not happen during the qualifying period:

  1. Discontinued for good reason: The use was lawful but was stopped before or during the qualifying period for a legitimate reason (financial hardship, equipment breakdown, death of owner, drought). The test is restrictive and objective.
  2. Steps taken in good faith: The use never actually happened, but it would have been lawful, and the person took reasonable, demonstrable steps toward implementing it before the NWA commenced (e.g., installing irrigation equipment, establishing plantations).

Section 33 is not a mechanism to revive long-abandoned water use or to create new entitlements. Each application is assessed on its own merits.

Section 34 — Authority to Continue

Provides the legal bridge: a person with ELU may continue using water under the same conditions until that use is replaced by a licence. Without Section 34, there would be a gap between the old and new systems.

Section 35 — Verification Process

This is the operational section that the system implements. It empowers DWS to require persons claiming ELU to apply for verification. It determines both the existence and extent (volume) of the ELU. Persons who do not apply when requested may not continue to use the water. Successful verification results in a Section 35(4) certificate. Dissatisfied water users may appeal to the Water Tribunal.

3.3 Section 53: Enforcement Directives

Section 53 is separate from the verification process — it is the enforcement mechanism that gives DWS the power to compel compliance through directives. In the V&V context:

SituationDWS ActionLegal Basis
Water user ignores Letter 1 (notice to apply)Issue Letter 1A — directive compelling the user to applyS53(1)
Water user ignores Letter 2 (request for information)Issue Letter 2A — directive compelling the user to provide informationS35(1) read with S53(1)
Verification reveals unlawful water useIssue Letter 4A — notice of intent to direct the user to stopS53(1)
After Letter 4A notice period expiresIssue Letters 4 & 5 — directive to cease unlawful useS53(1)
The Distinction
Section 35 is the process. Section 53 is the enforcement mechanism. Section 35 asks. Section 53 compels.

3.4 The Qualifying Period

The qualifying period is the 2-year window immediately before the NWA commenced on 1 October 1998:

Water SourceQualifying PeriodRationale
Surface water (rivers, dams, irrigation)1 October 1997 – 1 October 1999Section 32(1) — 2 years before commencement
Groundwater (boreholes)1 October 1996 – 1 October 1998Groundwater outside proclaimed areas was "private water" under the 1956 Act

The qualifying period is the evidentiary anchor for every V&V determination. All satellite imagery evidence must originate from or relate to this window. GIS digitising compares qualifying-period fields and dams against current-period fields and dams. The delta between qualifying and current use determines what is lawful, what is unlawful, and what represents an increase or decrease.

Critical Rule
Only circumstances and factors that existed prior to the commencement of the NWA (1 October 1998) may be considered in determining whether a water use qualifies as ELU. Subsequent behaviour by a water user is irrelevant to the determination.

3.6 PAJA Compliance

The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA, Act 3 of 2000) governs all administrative decisions in South Africa, including ELU determinations. Every ELU determination is an administrative action that must be lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair. Water users whose rights are adversely affected have a constitutional right to be given reasons.

Court and tribunal cases against DWS have mostly been lost due to procedural failures, not substantive errors. The system must therefore:

  • Record every state transition, letter issuance, and decision in an immutable audit log
  • Provide the water user with opportunity to respond at each stage
  • Support the appeal process (Water Tribunal referral)
  • Maintain traceable calculations and evidence to reconstruct how every ELU volume was determined

6.5 Workflow Engine Design

The workflow engine is implemented as a state machine with the following characteristics:

  • State storage: FileMaster.WorkflowStateId (FK) → WorkflowState table (seeded with all 21 control points + Section 35 letter states)
  • Transition guards: Each state transition is governed by a list of ITransitionGuard implementations that check evidence requirements and role permissions
  • Audit: Every state transition writes to the immutable AuditLog (who, what, when, previous state, new state, notes)
  • API: The engine exposes CanTransition(user, fileMaster, targetState) which returns a boolean and a reason string
  • Notifications: Domain events fire on successful transitions, triggering the Notification Service

7. Calculator Engine

7.1 Purpose and Scope

The Calculator Engine is a dedicated, parameterised module that performs all scientific and volumetric calculations required by the V&V process. It is implemented as a separate service (ICalculatorEngine) for the following reasons:

  • Maintainability: Calculation logic changes independently of workflow or UI logic. Formula updates (e.g., revised shape factors, new SAPWAT calibrations) can be deployed without affecting other modules.
  • Parameterisation: All constants (shape factors, catchment calibration data, scenario definitions) are stored as configuration, not hard-coded.
  • Testability: Calculations can be unit-tested in isolation with known inputs and expected outputs.
  • Auditability: Every calculation run is logged with its inputs, parameters, and outputs, providing a traceable evidence chain for Water Tribunal proceedings.
  • Reusability: The same calculation logic serves both the internal application (batch processing) and the public portal (read-only result display).

7.2 SAPWAT Calculator

SAPWAT is the industry-standard model for estimating crop water requirements in South Africa. The calculator takes crop type, irrigation system, and field area as inputs and produces a water requirement in mm/ha/a, which is then converted to total cubic metres.

InputSource

Output: SAPWATCalculationResult (mm/ha/a) stored on each FieldAndCrop record, plus the derived total volume in m³.

7.4 Dam Volume Calculator

Two calculation methods are supported, per Appendix D of the DWS requirements:

MethodFormulaWhen Used
Method 1 (Wall Length)Slope = R1/C1; Depth = Fetch/Slope; Capacity = Wall Length × Fetch × Depth × Factor / 2When wall length and contour data are available
Method 2 (Area)Capacity = Area(ha) × Depth(m) × Factor × 1000When dam surface area is known from GIS

Shape Factors: Triangle/ravine = 0.33; More square with bends = 0.4; More circular = 0.5

7.5 SFRA Calculator

Calculates stream flow reduction from forestry activities. Compares qualifying-period hectares and volumes against current-period hectares and volumes. Forestry that existed within the qualifying period has ELU regardless of whether it had a forestry permit (confirmed by 2024 Constitutional Court ruling).

8.3 Service of Documents

The NWA Section 162 prescribes how legal notices must be served. Letter 1 has the strictest requirements:

For a Natural Person:

  1. Hand delivery to the person
  2. If not possible → hand delivery to a responsible individual at their business or residential address
  3. If not possible → registered mail
  4. If address unknown → publish in the Government Gazette and a local newspaper

For a Juristic Person:

  1. Hand delivery to a responsible individual at the registered address or principal place of business
  2. If not possible → conspicuously attach to the main entrance
  3. If not possible → hand delivery to any member of the board of directors

The system records the serving official's name, the physical delivery date, and the agreed method for subsequent communication.

10. Electronic Signature Service

The Signature Service provides legally sound electronic signature capture for Section 35 letters. The process flow is:

  1. Letter Service generates a PDF document
  2. A SignatureRequest is created, targeting either an internal user (RegionalManager) or a public portal user
  3. The recipient is notified and presented with a signing page
  4. SignaturePad.js captures the signature on an HTML canvas
  5. The system records: signature image (PNG), timestamp, IP address, user agent, and the SHA-256 hash of the document at the time of signing
  6. A DigitalSignature record is created with the signature hash for integrity verification
  7. The signature image is embedded into the PDF, and the letter's status advances to "Signed"

This approach provides cryptographic proof that the document has not been modified after signing (by comparing the document hash at signing against the current document hash).

11. Public Portal

11.1 Purpose and Scope

The Public Portal is a secure, externally-facing module that gives water users direct visibility into how their validation case is being handled. It replaces the current practice of water users having to contact DWS by telephone or in writing to learn the status of their case.

The portal operates under a separate security realm from the internal application, with its own identity store, authentication cookie scheme, and session management.

11.2 Registration and Identity Verification

External users follow a controlled registration process:

  1. User visits the registration page and enters: name, ID number, email, phone, and optionally a property reference
  2. System sends an email verification link; user confirms their email
  3. System auto-matches the user to an existing PropertyOwner record (by ID number or email)
  4. If no auto-match is found, a DWS officer manually reviews and links the account
  5. MFA is mandatory (TOTP authenticator app or SMS OTP) before the user can access any case data
  6. User's account is activated and they can see their linked properties on the dashboard

11.4 Security and Data Isolation

  • Separate identity store: PublicUser is a separate entity, not inheriting from ApplicationUser or IdentityUser
  • Separate authentication cookie: Internal and external sessions cannot overlap
  • Row-level data isolation: All portal queries are always scoped by PublicUserId — an external user can never see records for a property not linked to their account
  • MFA mandatory: No case data is accessible without multi-factor authentication
  • Shorter session timeout: External sessions expire faster than internal sessions
  • DWS must approve links: A DWS Validator or above must approve the PublicUserProperty link before the user can see any case data

14. Audit and Compliance

Given the PAJA requirements and the likelihood of Water Tribunal challenges, the system implements a comprehensive, immutable audit trail:

AspectImplementation
Entity changesEvery create, update, and delete on audited entities writes an AuditLog record with old and new values (JSON), the user, timestamp, and IP address
Workflow transitionsEvery state change records the previous state, new state, who triggered it, when, and any notes or validation errors
Letter lifecycleGeneration, signing, issuance, delivery, response — each stage is timestamped and attributed to a user
Calculation runsEvery Calculator Engine execution records its inputs, parameters, and outputs for reproducibility
Portal activityDocument uploads, comment submissions, protest lodgings, letter views, and acknowledgments are all recorded
ImmutabilityAuditLog records can only be inserted, never updated or deleted. The database schema enforces this through permissions.
Tribunal Defence
When an ELU determination is challenged at the Water Tribunal, the system must be able to reconstruct the complete decision chain: what evidence was gathered, what calculations were performed, what communications were sent, what responses were received, and who made each decision at each step. The audit trail provides this.
