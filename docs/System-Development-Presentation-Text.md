## Slides 1–3: BACKGROUND

The enactment and implementation of the National Water Act, 1998 (NWA) gave recognition of the water uses that were authorised by the previous pieces of legislation.

In terms of section 32 of the Act, all water users or any successor in title, who has an existing lawful use authorised by or under any law which was in force two years immediately before the date of commencement of the Act. And in terms of section 34, this use could continue with such use under the same terms and conditions attached to such use or until it is replaced by a licence.

The Department requested users to register their water uses as exercised, regardless of the legal status thereof. The call to register water uses was made in terms of Section 3(b) of Regulation 1352 dated 12 November 1999.

The water use registration information as submitted by users was captured on the Department's Water Authorisation and Registration Management System (WARMS) Database.

Section 4(4) of the NWA replaces the water rights under old legislation with entitlements under the new legislation. However, existing water uses were allowed to continue as "existing lawful water use". The following provisions of the NWA define and limit the extent of this entitlement:

- **Section 32** defines existing lawful use as a water use that was lawfully undertaken during a two-year period immediately before the date of commencement of the NWA.
- **Section 33** allows for the declaration of any water use not considered under Section 32, as an existing lawful use.
- **Section 34** provides authority to continue with an existing lawful use under similar conditions until its replacement by a license.
- **Section 35** outlines provisions for persons claiming an entitlement to existing lawful use, to apply for the verification of the extent of existing lawful use. Water users may not continue to use the water if they do not apply for verification when requested to do so, or if the verification application has been refused.

### S32(1) Definition of Existing Lawful Water Use

S32(1) of NWA defines an Existing Lawful Water Use as a water use:

(a) which has Lawfully taken place at any time during a period of two years immediately before the date of commencement of this Act (NWA) and which—
  (i) was authorized by or under any law which was in force immediately before the date of commencement of this Act.
  (ii) is a stream flow reduction activity contemplated in section 36(1); or
  (iii) is a controlled activity contemplated in section 37(1); or

(b) which has been declared an existing lawful water use under section 33

## Slide 5: Continuation of ELU — Section 34

S34(1) A person or that person's successor-in-title, may continue with an existing lawful water use, subject to:
- Any existing conditions or obligations attaching to that use;
- Its replacement by a licence in terms of this Act, or
- Any other limitation or prohibition by or under this Act

S34(2) A responsible authority may, subject to any regulation made under section 26(1)(c), require the registration of an existing lawful water use.

---

## Slide 6: Section 35 — Verification Process

S35(1) The responsible authority may in order to verify the lawfulness or extent of an existing water use, by written notice require any person claiming an entitlement to that water use to apply for a verification of that use.

(2) A notice under subsection (1) must:
- (a) have a suitable application form annexed to it;
- (b) specify a date before which the application must be submitted;
- (c) inform the person concerned that any entitlement to continue with the water use may lapse if an application is not made on or before the specified date; and
- (d) be delivered personally or sent by registered mail to the person concerned.

(3) A responsible authority—
- (a) may require the applicant at the applicant's expense to obtain and provide it with other information, in addition to the information contained in the application;
- (b) may conduct its own investigation into the veracity and the lawfulness of the water use in question;
- (c) may invite written comments from any person who has an interest in the matter; and
- (d) must afford the applicant an opportunity to make representations on any aspect of the application.

## Slides 15–20: Application of Legal Principles for S21(a) — Abstraction

### Types of Authorisations
- Riparian Land — Riparian Right
- Private Water
- Permit
- Additional Proof of Lawfulness:
  - Servitudes (water linked)
  - Title Deeds (with notes regarding water right(s))
  - Sales Agreements (e.g., Water Rations)
  - Farmers Agreements (Formalized)
  - Court Orders
- **Proclamations:**
  - Government Water Control Areas (Surface Water)
  - Subterranean Government Water Control Areas (Groundwater)

### Riparian Land — Surplus and Normal Flow
- **Surplus Water**: Every riparian owner could use as much surplus water on riparian land as could be beneficially used. Such use need not be curtailed in favour of a downstream owner unless there is an agreement to the contrary and can be impounded and stored.
- **Normal Flow**: A riparian landowner is entitled to the reasonable use of a fair share of the normal flow of a public stream for agricultural and urban purposes. A riparian owner's share of the normal flow could be determined by the Water Court.
- NB: All lawful agreements form part of the investigation in making a determination of an existing lawful water use.

### Abstraction of Water: Public Stream (Section 9B)
- **From 1971**: limits imposed — no person could construct, alter or enlarge any water work in which more than 4 cubic feet (113 litres per second) could be diverted.
- **From 1975**: A riparian owner could abstract a maximum of 110 litres per second from a public stream without a permit (on land registered in deeds registry in 1975).
- **From 1987**: Maximum of 110 litres per second (based on 'fair share' approach and subject to availability) without a permit. The 110 l/s can be varied in terms of Section 9B(1C) in a defined area.

### Subdivisions of Riparian Land
- Riparian land remains riparian even if a subdivision removed access to a stream.
- The owner of a subdivision is entitled to a share of the water as the owners may agree or as determined by the Water Court.
- The date of subdivision is the date of registration in the Deeds Registry.

### Private Water (Section 5 of the Water Act)
- Private water is all water which rises or falls naturally on any land or naturally drains or is led on to one or more pieces of land which are subject to separate grants, but not capable of common use for irrigation.
- Private water is for the sole and exclusive use and enjoyment of the landowner.
- Private water could be controlled in a Government Water Control Area.

### Example: Blyde River GWCA Proclamation
- Established in terms of Section 59(1)(b) of the Water Act, 1956 by Proclamation 67 of 1935
- Riparian owners may irrigate 30 hectares or 40% of irrigable land (whichever is greater)
- Existing lawful irrigation larger than minimum allocation can continue with max 53% of available irrigable land
- Maximum rate of abstraction: 9 900 m³/ha
- Maximum storage capacity: 5 000 m³/ha, max 50 000 m³/property

## Slides 21–22: Application of Legal Principles for S21(b) — Storage

### Storage — Public Water (Section 9B)
- **From 1971**: Limits imposed — max 100 morgen feet (261 000 cubic meters) storage capacity.
- **From 1975**: Max 250 000 cubic meters without a permit (on land registered in deeds registry in 1975).
- **From 1987**: Max 250 000 m³ without a permit. Can be varied in terms of Section 9B(1C).

### Storage of Private Water
- Construction of a water work to impound or store private water was not generally allowed other than under the Soil Conservation Act, 1969.

### Storage in Protected Areas (Section 15 and 16)
- Riparian owner may store up to 114 000 m³ of surplus water and divert max 300 litres per second of surplus flow per property.

## Slide 23: Application of Legal Principles for S33(1) & (2) & (3)

### S33(2) — Kader Asmal Declaration — Scheduled Areas
- All scheduled areas under government and irrigation board schemes as of 1 January 1999 for which the prescribed rates have been paid up to 30 September 1998 are existing lawful water uses.
- Full scheduled and paid for volume of water is considered lawful.
- **Dormant but paid for volume of water is considered as ELU.**
- Scheduled water users who had not used water at the time were given three months to exercise the scheduled water use.

## Slides 24–28: Application of Legal Principles for S21(d) — SFRA

### Lawfulness of Stream Flow Reduction Activities

**Pre-1984** (no need for prior authorisation to plant):
- Any SFRA planted prior to the promulgation of Forestry Act 122 of 1984 are regarded as lawful, except if discontinued for more than 5 years before the 1984 Act.
- Genus planted prior to 1984 and still in existence during the qualifying period is the existing lawful water use.

**Post-1984** (Permit required prior to planting):
- SFRA was authorized through Permits after 26 May 1972 to 1999. Permits issued from 1984 are applicable.
- The extent of the permit issued, provided that the afforestation was established during the qualifying period, is the existing lawful water use.
- Any permit extent not established in the qualifying period is NOT included in existing lawful use.

**Homelands (pre-1994)**:
- All afforestation established in the former homeland areas is regarded as existing lawful water use.
- Genus planted prior to 1994 in Homelands and still in existence during the qualifying period is the existing lawful water use.

### The Technical and Legal Process

**Validation Process** (for determining water use extent — how much land was used):
- Focus on land used for afforestation during 1 Oct 1996 – 30 Sep 1998.
- Use scientific methods to confirm past use.
- Even land not fully planted but prepared for afforestation may qualify.
- Do NOT reduce land area based on changes in tree species.
- Official notices banning planting in certain areas may still apply.

**Verification Process** (for verifying water use lawfulness):
- Consider any legal restrictions that applied when the NWA started.
- Restrictions from old forestry laws before the 1984 Forest Act or from homeland (TBVC) laws are not valid.
- Afforestation before 1972 needed no permits, so no restrictions.
- Official notices banning planting in certain areas may still apply.

### If Authorized by Permit
- Compare extent permitted with extent during qualifying period:
  - If extents match → qualifying period extent + genus is the ELU
  - If qualifying > permitted → permitted extent is the ELU (CME must be informed of excess)
  - If qualifying < permitted → qualifying period extent + genus is the ELU
  - If no footprint during qualifying period → no ELU on the property
- Harvested fields/compartments during qualifying period are recognized as part of the existing extent.

### If Authorized as Pre-1972 Plantation
- Determine extent using Pre-1984 aerial photographs or closest to 1984.
- Extent of genus during qualifying period matching Pre-1984 is the ELU.
- If no footprint during qualifying period → zero ELU.
- Harvested fields/compartments during qualifying period are recognized as part of the existing extent.

## Slides 33–44: Process Steps (Technical Workflow)

### Obtain Spatial Information (Slide 34)
- SG Farm boundaries and subdivisions
- Catchment and Management boundaries
- Rivers and dams
- Scheme area, Government Control area boundaries
- Satellite Imagery (winter and summer months from 1996–1999)

- Topo cadastral images

### Cadastral Information (Slides 35–36)
- Cleaning and updating data from Cadastral and Deeds information
- Access Registrar of Title Deeds and Chief Surveyor General
- Align properties that were sub-divided/consolidated
- Cadastral Edits

### WARMS Information (Slide 37)
- Previous Field Surveys & Data
- Government Water Control Area & Date
- Irrigation Board
- Section 9B
- Government Water Scheme
- Part of an original riparian farm

### Property & Source Evaluation (Slide 38)
- Obtain Deeds report, Title Deeds and SG Diagram
- Evaluate Title Deed Owner and Register in WARMS Database
- Evaluate property on first registration date, subdivisions, consolidations
- Compare SG Diagram property size and diagram with GIS
- Evaluate source as per WARMS to determine ELU
- Evaluate property location with regards to: GWCA & Date, Irrigation Board, Section 9B, Government Water Scheme, Part of original riparian farm

### Additional Information (Slide 39)
Types: Permit, Section 32/33 Approval, Transfer (Temporary/Permanent), Ad-hoc Field Survey, Water Court, Other Water Act, License, General Authorisation, Other

### GIS Analysis and Field Digitising (Slide 40)
- Identify property on GIS
- Overlay property boundaries on qualifying and current period satellite images
- Evaluate occurrences of irrigation fields or storage structures
- Evaluate winter and summer images for qualifying period
- Compare GIS analysis with WARMS registration
- Digitise crop fields and storage structures
- Calculate field hectares
- Calculate dam storage
- Create GIS maps for qualifying and current periods

### Field and Crop + SAPWAT Modelling (Slide 41)
Complete field and crop information with:
- Satellite image used with reference and date
- Survey date, Field Areas, Crop name, Plant date, Rotation factor
- Irrigation System, Water source (Type, name and %)
- Crop area and SAPWAT (mm/ha/a)
- Use as input to SAPWAT modelling to determine volume usage

### Dam Volume Calculations (Slides 42–43)
- Depth = Fetch / Slope
- Slope = River distance between two nearest contours / contour difference
- Capacity = Wall Length × Fetch × Depth × Factor
- Factors: Triangular = 0.33, More square with bend = 0.4, More circular = 0.5
- Or: Area (ha) × Depth (m) × Factor × 1000
